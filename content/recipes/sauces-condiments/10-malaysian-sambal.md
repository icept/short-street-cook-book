---
title: Malaysian Sambal
serves: 4–6
time: 30 minutes, plus 30 minutes soaking and 35 minutes cooking
---

Penang has some of the best food I have eaten anywhere, and this is my attempt to carry a bit of it home. The long simmer at the end is the thing — it transforms raw blended paste into something deeply layered and savoury. Make a batch, keep it in the fridge, and use it with everything.

## Ingredients

- 30 dried chillies
- 20 Thai chillies
- 5 garlic cloves
- 5 cm fresh turmeric
- 5 cm galangal
- 1 stalk lemongrass, sliced
- 150 g shallots, sliced
- 1 tbsp shrimp paste (belacan)
- 1 tbsp fish sauce
- 3 tbsp palm sugar
- 2 tbsp tamarind paste
- 80 ml vegetable oil
- 250 ml water

## Method

1. Soak the dried chillies in boiling water for 30 minutes until soft. Drain.
2. Blend the soaked dried chillies with the Thai chillies, turmeric, galangal, garlic, lemongrass, and shallots until a smooth paste forms.
3. Stir in the shrimp paste, fish sauce, tamarind paste, and palm sugar.
4. Heat the oil in a wok over medium-high heat. Tip in the paste and fry for 2–3 minutes until fragrant and deepened in colour.
5. Pour in the water and simmer over low heat for 30 minutes or more, stirring occasionally, until the oil separates to the surface and the sambal is thick and rich.

## Flavour

Hot, sweet, sour, and deeply savoury all at once. The tamarind gives it its characteristic brightness, the palm sugar rounds off the edge, and the shrimp paste and fish sauce provide a backbone of umami that holds the whole thing together.

## Why it works

The initial fry is the key step — cooking the blended paste in hot oil drives off moisture and starts building depth that no amount of simmering alone can replicate. The long, slow simmer then integrates everything and concentrates the paste. Watch for the oil separating to the surface; that is your signal it is ready.

## Substitutions

**Palm sugar** — brown sugar or jaggery work fine; the flavour shifts slightly sweeter and less complex.

**Galangal** — fresh ginger is the standard substitute; the result is sharper and less piney.

**Shrimp paste** — omit for a vegetarian version, or replace with a teaspoon of miso; the depth will be different but still good.

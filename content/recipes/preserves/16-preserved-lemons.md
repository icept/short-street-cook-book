---
title: Preserved Lemons
yield: 1 jar (8–10 lemons)
time: 15 minutes, plus 4 weeks curing
---

One jar of preserved lemons at the back of the fridge changes what you can do
in the kitchen for months. The rind is the point — rinse it, scrape out the
pulp, and use it wherever you want a deep, fermented citrus hit without
fresh acidity. It's particularly good in braises, on roasted vegetables, in
dressings, and stirred through yoghurt. The [lemons](/produce/01-fruit/01-lemon/)
off the tree are the obvious starting point, and the glut in winter gives you
every reason to make a batch.

## Ingredients

- 8–10 unwaxed [lemons](/produce/01-fruit/01-lemon/), plus 4–5 extra for juicing
- 200 g fine non-iodised salt
- 1 tbsp sugar (optional — softens the cure slightly)

### Optional aromatics
- 2–3 dried [bay leaves](/produce/02-herbs/02-bay/)
- 1 tsp black peppercorns
- 1 cinnamon stick
- 3–4 dried chillies

## Method

1. Scrub the lemons under warm water. Do not peel.
2. Cut each lemon as if quartering it, but stop 1–2 cm before cutting
   all the way through the base — the lemon should open out into four
   segments but stay attached at the bottom.
3. Pack a generous pinch of salt (about a tablespoon) into the centre of
   each lemon, pressing the cut surfaces together to hold it in.
4. Pack the salted lemons tightly into a sterilised wide-mouth jar, pressing
   down firmly so they begin to release their juice.
5. Add any aromatics you're using, tucking them between the lemons.
6. Squeeze the extra lemons and pour the juice over to cover the fruit
   completely. If there isn't quite enough juice, top up with a little
   extra salt water (1 tbsp salt per 100 ml water).
7. Seal the jar. Leave at room temperature for 24 hours, then turn the jar
   upside down and back each day for the first week to redistribute the salt
   and juices.
8. After a week, move to a cool, dark spot. They are ready to use after
   four weeks. Refrigerate once opened.

## Using preserved lemons

**Rinse the rind** under cold water before using and scrape out the soft
pulp and pith — it's too salty and bitter to use directly. Only the rind
goes into the dish.

**Finely sliced or chopped**, the rind works in: braises (especially lamb
and chicken), on roasted cauliflower and root vegetables, in a vinaigrette
with olive oil, stirred into hummus, or alongside grilled fish.

**A little goes a long way.** Start with a strip or two and add more to taste.

## Notes

**Waxed vs unwaxed:** supermarket lemons are often waxed. If using them,
scrub well with a stiff brush under hot water, or blanch briefly.
Own-tree lemons need only a rinse.

**Shelf life:** keep in the fridge once opened; they last for up to a year.
A thin layer of olive oil over the top of the jar helps preserve them.

**Variations:** Moroccan preserved lemons sometimes include spices in the
jar (the optional aromatics above). A plainer cure with just salt gives
a cleaner flavour that's more versatile.

---
title: Home-Cured Olives
yield: 1 kg cured olives (scales with your harvest)
time: 20 minutes active, plus 5–8 weeks curing
---

The [olive tree](/produce/03-olives/) drops its fruit in April and May, and
this is what we do with it. The cure is slow rather than difficult — most of
the time is waiting, with five minutes of work each day to change the water.
The end result is genuinely better than anything from a jar: firmer, cleaner,
and tasting of the particular olives off your particular tree.

The process has two distinct phases. The water cure draws out the bitterness
over two to three weeks. The brine firms the olive and develops the final
flavour over the following weeks. Don't skip or rush either stage.

## Ingredients

### Water cure phase
- 1 kg fresh [olives](/produce/03-olives/), any stage from green to black
- Fresh cold water to cover

### Brine phase
- 1 litre cold water
- 100 g non-iodised salt (10% brine)
- 100 ml white wine vinegar or apple cider vinegar

### To finish (optional)
- 3–4 garlic cloves, sliced
- 1 fresh or dried chilli, split
- Zest of 1 [lemon](/produce/01-fruit/01-lemon/) or a strip of [Cara Cara](/produce/01-fruit/02-cara-cara-orange/) orange peel
- 2–3 [bay leaves](/produce/02-herbs/02-bay/)
- Olive oil to cover

## Method

### Phase 1 — Water cure (2–3 weeks)

1. Sort the olives and discard any that are bruised, split, or damaged.
2. Using a sharp knife, slit each olive once lengthways through to the stone.
   This is the slow step — work steadily, not in a rush.
3. Pack the olives into a clean, non-reactive container (glass, ceramic, or
   food-grade plastic). Cover completely with cold fresh water and weight the
   olives down with a plate or zip-lock bag filled with water to keep them
   submerged.
4. Each day, drain and replace with fresh cold water. Do not skip a day —
   stale water slows the cure and can cause off-flavours.
5. Start tasting after ten days. The olives are ready to move to the brine
   when the bitterness has reduced significantly — they should taste sharp
   and slightly tangy but no longer unpleasantly bitter. This takes two to
   three weeks depending on the ripeness of the fruit; greener olives take
   longer than riper ones.

### Phase 2 — Brine cure (3–5 weeks)

6. Dissolve the salt completely in the water, then stir in the vinegar.
7. Pack the olives into sterilised glass jars. Pour the brine over to cover
   completely. Weight down as before to keep olives submerged.
8. Seal the jars loosely (to allow any fermentation gases to escape) and
   leave at cool room temperature, out of direct sunlight.
9. Taste after two weeks. Continue for up to five weeks total, tasting every
   few days. The olives are ready when the bitterness is fully gone and the
   flavour is clean, savoury, and balanced.

### To finish

10. Drain the olives and taste. If they are slightly more salty than you'd like,
    rinse briefly under cold water.
11. Pack into clean jars. Add any flavourings — garlic, chilli, citrus peel,
    bay — layering them through the olives.
12. Cover completely with olive oil, tapping the jar to release any air pockets.
    Seal and refrigerate.
13. Leave for at least three to four days before eating to let the flavourings
    infuse. They keep in the fridge for several months.

## Notes

**The oil from the finished jar** takes on the flavours of the cure and is
excellent for cooking — use it to fry garlic, dress a salad, or finish a pasta.

**Mixed ripeness:** if your tree has both green and dark olives at harvest,
cure them together. The darker olives will finish the water-cure phase faster;
you can pull them out a few days before the green ones if needed.

**Scaling up:** this recipe scales directly. For every additional kilogram
of olives, make another litre of brine (100 g salt + 100 ml vinegar per litre).

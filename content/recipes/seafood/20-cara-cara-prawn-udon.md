---
title: Cara Cara Prawn Udon with Nam Jim
category: Seafood
serves: 2
time: 35 minutes, plus 1 hour marinating
---

Inspired by Yotam Ottolenghi's orange nam jim udon, adapted to use our Cara Cara oranges. The nam jim — a Thai-style dressing built on orange juice, tamarind, fish sauce, and toasted Aleppo chilli — is the kind of thing you'll want to make in double batches and keep in the fridge. Use the best prawns you can find; it matters here.

## Ingredients

### For the prawns
- 350g raw extra-large king prawns, peeled and deveined
- 1 small garlic clove, crushed
- 3 tbsp vegetable oil
- Salt

### For the nam jim
- 2 Cara Cara oranges — juice one for about 80ml, cut the other into wedges
- ½ tbsp basmati rice
- ¾ tbsp Aleppo chilli flakes
- 20g tamarind paste
- 40ml fish sauce
- 1 tbsp maple syrup
- 1 shallot, finely diced
- 5g coriander leaves and stems, finely chopped

### To serve
- 600g cooked udon noodles
- 10g Thai basil leaves
- 3 spring onions, finely sliced on the diagonal
- 10g coriander leaves
- 2 red chillies, deseeded and finely sliced
- 1 tbsp sesame seeds (black and white), toasted

## Method

1. Toss the prawns with the crushed garlic, 1 tbsp of the oil, and a pinch of salt. Leave to marinate for at least an hour in the fridge.

2. Pour the Cara Cara juice into a small saucepan and reduce over medium heat until you have about 60ml — this concentrates the flavour and removes some of the raw sweetness. Set aside to cool.

3. Toast the basmati rice in a dry pan over medium heat, stirring constantly, until golden and smelling nutty — about 3 minutes. Add the Aleppo chilli and toast for another 30 seconds. Tip into a mortar and grind to a coarse powder.

4. Combine the reduced orange juice, ground rice and chilli, tamarind paste, fish sauce, maple syrup, shallot, and chopped coriander. Stir well and taste — it should be sour, salty, a little sweet, and fragrant.

5. Heat the remaining 2 tbsp oil in a large frying pan over high heat. Fry the prawns in two batches, 2 minutes per side, until just cooked through and lightly charred at the edges. Don't crowd the pan.

6. Tip the noodles into the pan off the heat, add two-thirds of the nam jim and two-thirds of the garnish herbs and spring onion. Toss together until everything is well coated and the noodles are warmed through.

7. Divide between two bowls. Spoon over the remaining nam jim, scatter with the rest of the herbs, the sliced chilli, and the sesame seeds. Serve the Cara Cara wedges alongside for squeezing over.

## Flavour

The Cara Cara orange sits at the centre of the nam jim — its lower acidity and faint berry note make a rounder dressing than a standard navel would. Tamarind and fish sauce give it depth and salt; the toasted rice adds a dry, nutty texture that you wouldn't expect but can't do without.

## Why it works

Reducing the orange juice before building the dressing drives off the bright top notes and leaves something more complex behind. The ground rice absorbs some of the liquid in the nam jim and keeps the texture from being too loose on the noodles — it's a technique from north-east Thai cuisine, where toasted rice powder turns up in larb and similar dishes.

## Substitutions

**Cara Cara orange** — a regular navel orange works but the dressing will be sharper. Blood orange would be excellent when in season. Mandarin is too sweet and not juicy enough.

**Aleppo chilli** — substitute gochugaru (Korean chilli flakes) at the same quantity for a similar mild heat and slight sweetness. Regular dried chilli flakes will be hotter; use half.

**Udon** — fresh ramen or thick rice noodles both work. Dried udon, reconstituted, is fine if fresh isn't available.

**Maple syrup** — palm sugar (grated or dissolved) is more traditional; palm sugar is richer and less obviously sweet.

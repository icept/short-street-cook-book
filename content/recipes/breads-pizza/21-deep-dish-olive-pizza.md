---
title: Deep Dish Pizza with Home-Cured Olives
category: Breads & Pizza
serves: 4
time: 50 minutes, plus 30–45 minutes dough resting
---

A proper pan pizza built around the backyard olive cure — or, while the olives are still in the brine, a pork sausage version that holds its own. Beef tallow in the tin essentially fries the crust edge — deeper flavour and better crunch than olive oil alone.

## Ingredients

### For the base
- 1 ball Woolworths fresh pizza dough, at room temperature
- 1–2 tbsp beef tallow, for the tin
- Olive oil, for drizzling

### For the sauce
- 400 ml passata
- 2 garlic cloves, finely chopped
- Pinch of sugar
- Fresh [oregano](/produce/02-herbs/05-oregano/) and/or [basil](/produce/02-herbs/01-basil/), chopped
- Salt to taste — go easy, the olives or sausages carry salt

### For the topping
- 200–250 g shredded pizza cheese
- Zest of ½ [lemon](/produce/01-fruit/01-lemon/), to finish
- Chilli flakes, to finish (optional)

Then one of the following:

### Olive version
- 80 g whole [home-cured olives](/recipes/preserves/15-cured-olives/) (15–18 olives; about 65 g once pitted), pitted and halved — taste one first; rinse briefly if they're saltier than you'd like

### Sausage version (while the olives cure)
- 3 good pork sausages (about 210 g), casings removed — the McLoughlin Butchers Irish pork pack is the house pick
- ½ tsp fennel seeds, lightly crushed (optional — nudges them toward Italian sausage)

## Method

1. Take the dough out of the fridge 30–45 minutes ahead. Cold dough springs back and won't hold the shape of the tin.

2. Make the sauce. Sweat the garlic in a little oil, tip in the passata, sugar, and herbs, and simmer for 10–15 minutes until it's thick enough that a spoon dragged through leaves a clean line. Thin sauce is what ruins a deep dish — it soaks the crust.

3. **Sausage version only:** while the sauce simmers, squeeze the sausages from their casings into the dry skillet over medium-high heat and brown, breaking the meat into rough crumbles. Add the fennel seeds for the last minute if using. Tip out the crumbles and pour off the fat, but leave the browned residue in the pan — it seasons the crust.

4. Melt the tallow in the 20 cm cast-iron skillet (or a 23–26 cm cake tin) and swirl to coat the base and sides generously. Heat the oven to 200°C with the rack in the bottom third.

5. Press the dough into the tin and 3–4 cm up the sides. Layer in this order: nearly all the cheese directly on the dough, the olives or sausage crumbles scattered over the cheese, the thick sauce spooned over the top, then the remaining cheese and a drizzle of olive oil to finish.

6. Bake for 25–30 minutes until the edge is deep golden brown and the sauce is bubbling. The tallow-fried edge should release cleanly from the tin.

7. Rest for 5–10 minutes in the tin before lifting out and cutting, or the slices will slump. Finish with the lemon zest and chilli flakes.

## Flavour

Rich and deeply savoury — fried crust, molten cheese, and a concentrated tomato sauce. The olives run salty and sharp through the middle; the sausage version trades that edge for something rounder and fennel-sweet. The lemon zest at the end earns its place in both — it bridges the topping and the tomato. Don't skip it.

## Why it works

Cheese under the sauce is the deep dish move: it shields the crust from moisture so the base stays crisp under a heavy topping. The tallow does the rest — it half-fries the edge in the tin, which olive oil alone won't manage. One tablespoon gives crisp; two gives properly fried, almost focaccia-like edges. Both are correct. For the sausage version, browning first matters: raw sausage baked into the pie leaks grease into the crust, while browned crumbles arrive already rendered.

## Substitutions

**Beef tallow** — olive oil works, but the edge crisps rather than fries and the flavour is lighter.

**Home-cured olives** — olives a week or two into the [brine phase](/recipes/preserves/15-cured-olives/) are ideal: firm, lightly salted, and they hold their shape in the oven. Finished oil-packed olives work too — taste one and rinse if needed, and salt the sauce even more cautiously. A good bought Kalamata, rinsed, is the off-season fallback — or just make the sausage version.

**Pork sausages** — any decent butcher's pork sausage works here. A proper Italian fennel sausage is the shortcut: skip the fennel seeds and brown it as is.

**Dough** — one Woolies ball fills the house 20 cm skillet with proper deep sides; the same ball stretches to a 23 cm tin with a thinner base. For a 28 cm or larger pan, use one and a half.

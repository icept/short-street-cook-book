One of the self-sufficient herbs — plant it once and it spreads, needs
almost no water, and crops year-round. The flavour is warmest in summer
after the plant has been in the sun; the dried version is actually more
useful than fresh in long-cooked dishes, where the fresh can become
slightly grassy and sharp.

## In season

Year-round. At its most aromatic and full-flavoured in summer. A vigorous
grower in Perth's climate; cut it back hard once or twice a year to keep
it from getting woody.

## Pairs with

- Tomato — dried oregano in a tomato sauce is the Italian baseline
- Garlic and olive oil — in a marinade or drizzled over roasted vegetables
- Lemon — in a Greek-style dressing or marinade
- Lamb — especially in a kebab or braised dish with tomato
- Feta — scattered over a Greek salad
- Courgette and eggplant — oregano suits all the Mediterranean vegetables
- Capers and olives — in a Sicilian-style sauce

## Where to get it locally

- Own plant
- Dried in bulk at **Kakulas Bros** (Northbridge) — the dried version
  is worth buying good stock of; it keeps for a year and is better value
  than supermarket packets

## Notes

**Drying:** tie small bundles and hang in a dry spot, or strip the leaves
and dry flat on a tray. Strip and jar when brittle. Home-dried oregano
from a well-grown plant is noticeably better than most commercial dried herb.

**Fresh vs dried:** fresh is best used in salads and dressings added
at the end; dried suits anything that cooks for more than 20 minutes.

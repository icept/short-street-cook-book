The most used herb in the patch by a distance. Thyme goes into almost
everything that takes more than fifteen minutes on the heat — stocks, braises,
roasts, soups. It's forgiving: it handles long cooking without going bitter,
works fresh or dried, and pairs with almost any protein or vegetable without
dominating.

## In season

Year-round. Low-maintenance, drought-tolerant, and happy in Perth's conditions.
Harvest sprigs freely; cutting the plant back prevents it going woody.

## Pairs with

- Bay — the two are inseparable in a bouquet garni; almost everything
  slow-cooked benefits from both
- Garlic — in every braise, roast, and stock
- Lemon — lemon thyme specifically is excellent; regular thyme and lemon zest
  work equally well together
- Chicken — under the skin or in the pan during roasting
- Mushrooms — in a cream sauce or on toast with good butter
- Pork — in a rub, a brine, or alongside during slow cooking
- Root vegetables — in a pot roast or roasting tin alongside carrots and turnips

## Where to get it locally

- Own plant
- Potted thyme is widely available at nurseries and supermarket herb sections
- Lemon thyme is worth seeking out as a separate plant — it's a different
  cultivar and the lemon note is real, not incidental

## Notes

**Strip the leaves:** hold the sprig at the top and run your fingers down
toward the base — the leaves strip off cleanly. For slow cooking, add
the whole sprig and remove it before serving.

**Drying:** small bundles hung in a dry spot. Dried thyme is a reliable
substitute in most cooked applications; use about half the quantity.

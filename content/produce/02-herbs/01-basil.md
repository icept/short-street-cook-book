A summer herb that needs warmth to be worth growing. In Perth the season is
long — plant out after the last cool snap in October and it runs through to
March — but don't try to push it through winter. The flavour collapses when
the nights cool. Pick leaves from the top to encourage branching; once it
flowers, the leaves turn bitter.

## In season

November through March. Needs full sun and warmth. Bolts quickly as
daylight shortens in late summer; harvest generously through the peak.

## Pairs with

- Tomato — the foundational pairing; raw tomatoes and torn basil
- Garlic and olive oil — in a pesto or simply drizzled
- Mozzarella and burrata — caprese; the cheese needs good basil to be worth eating
- Pine nuts and parmesan — for a classic pesto Genovese
- Peach and stone fruit — basil with peach and mozzarella is underused
- Lemon — in a vinaigrette or to finish a pasta
- Chilli — Thai basil especially, in stir-fries and curries

## Where to get it locally

- Own patch in summer
- Seedlings and bunches at **Stirling Farmers Market** (Sundays) through spring
  and summer
- Potted plants at most hardware stores and nurseries in spring — easier
  than growing from seed

## Notes

**Don't refrigerate fresh basil** — cold blackens the leaves. Keep a bunch
stem-down in a glass of water on the bench.

**Basil oil:** blanch briefly, squeeze dry, blend with olive oil, strain.
Freeze in ice-cube trays. Better than pesto for maintaining the bright
colour.

**Thai basil** is a different plant — anise-scented rather than sweet, and
it holds up to heat. Worth growing alongside the Italian variety.

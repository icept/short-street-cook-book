Dill is worth growing in the cooler months even if you only ever use it with
fish, yoghurt, and cucumbers — that combination is enough to justify the
space. The feathery fronds are delicate and best used raw or added at the
last minute; heat destroys the flavour fast.

## In season

April through September. Like coriander and parsley, it prefers cool weather
and bolts the moment the heat arrives. A successive sowing in autumn and
again in late winter stretches the season.

## Pairs with

- Salmon and trout — the classic; dill butter, dill cream, gravlax cure
- Cucumber — in a yoghurt sauce, a pickle, or just dressed with vinegar
- Yoghurt and crème fraîche — in sauces, dips, and dressings
- New potatoes — boiled with dill is a Scandinavian classic worth adopting
- Eggs — in a frittata or scrambled with smoked fish
- Mustard — dill and mustard together in a Scandinavian-style dressing
- Capers — in a tartare sauce or alongside cured fish

## Where to get it locally

- Own patch in cooler months
- Bunches occasionally at **Stirling Farmers Market** in winter
- Potted plants at most nurseries in autumn

## Notes

**Add at the last minute:** dill loses its character quickly once heated.
Add to warm dishes just before serving rather than cooking it through.

**Freeze the fronds:** roughly chop and freeze flat in a bag. Frozen dill
is usable in cooked applications, though not for garnishing raw.

**Dill seed:** if you let the plant bolt and flower, collect the seeds —
they're milder than caraway and good in pickling brines.

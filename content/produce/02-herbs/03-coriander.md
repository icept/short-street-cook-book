People are divided on coriander, but if you grow your own you can use the
whole plant — leaves, stems, and roots — which is a different proposition
to the bunches that arrive half-wilted from the supermarket. The stems
and roots have the most concentrated flavour and hold up to heat; the
leaves are strictly for finishing.

## In season

Cooler months — roughly April through October. Bolts rapidly when
temperatures rise, running straight to seed. Plant in autumn for a
long productive season; a late-summer planting is short-lived.

## Pairs with

- Chilli and lime — the Southeast Asian baseline
- Fish sauce and garlic — in Thai and Vietnamese cooking
- Ginger — in both fresh and dried form
- Coconut milk — in curries and laksa
- Avocado — in guacamole and fresh salsas
- Cumin and coriander seed — the whole plant and its dried seeds together
- Yoghurt — as a cooling garnish for spiced dishes

## Where to get it locally

- Own patch in cooler months
- Year-round bunches at Asian grocers and most supermarkets — look for
  bunches with the roots still attached for maximum value
- **Kakulas Bros** (Northbridge) stocks coriander seed in bulk

## Used in

- [Cara Cara Prawn Udon with Nam Jim](/recipes/seafood/20-cara-cara-prawn-udon/) — leaves and stems in the nam jim and as a garnish

## Notes

**Use the whole plant:** rinse and roughly chop the roots and lower stems
for pastes and curry bases. Reserve the leaves for garnishing.

**Letting it go to seed:** if a plant bolts, let it flower and set seed —
coriander seeds are a useful spice and dry well on the stem.

**Storing:** stand the bunch in a glass of water in the fridge, loosely
covered. Will last a week. Don't wash until ready to use.

Mint grows itself — plant it once and the real task is keeping it contained.
It spreads by underground runners and will take over a bed if left unchecked;
a buried pot or a dedicated container keeps it honest. The flavour of fresh
garden spearmint is cleaner and less medicinal than anything from a bag.

## In season

Year-round in Perth, though it dies back slightly in the coldest weeks of
winter and comes away strongest in spring and summer. Pick sprigs freely
from the top to encourage bushy growth; letting it flower weakens the leaf
flavour.

## Pairs with

- Lamb — the classic; mint sauce, mint jelly, or fresh leaves in the pan
- Yoghurt — raita, tzatziki, or a simple spoon of yoghurt with chopped mint
- Cucumber — in a salad, a drink, or alongside cold fish
- Lemon and lime — in drinks, dressings, and desserts
- Pea — fresh peas with mint and butter is a seasonal thing worth doing
- Watermelon and stone fruit — mint scattered over is a simple improvement
- Middle Eastern spices — with cumin, coriander, and cinnamon in a lamb dish
- Rum and spirits — in a Mojito, a Mint Julep, or a stirred-down highball

## Where to get it locally

- Own plant
- Potted spearmint widely available at supermarket herb sections and nurseries
- Fresh bunches at most supermarkets and Asian grocers year-round

## Notes

**Contain it:** plant in a buried pot or a standalone container. Mint
runners spread aggressively underground and will take over a garden bed
within a season.

**Reviving cut mint:** trim the stem ends and stand in a glass of cold
water in the fridge. Wilted mint usually recovers in an hour or two.

**Mint oil and drying:** mint loses a lot of character when dried or
cooked through. Use it fresh wherever possible; add it at the very end
of any warm dish.

**Vietnamese mint** (rau răm) is a different plant entirely — spicier and
more pungent, good in salads and alongside pho. Worth growing separately
if you cook a lot of Southeast Asian food.

Ours is nearly a hedge. Rosemary grows itself in Perth — plant it once in
a sunny, well-drained spot and it will outlast most other things in the
garden. The resinous, piney quality is the point, but it's strong; a
single sprig is often enough. Use it with restraint and it adds something
distinctive; overdo it and it smells like furniture polish.

## In season

Year-round. Picks freely at any time; flavour is most concentrated in
the warmer months. Cut the plant back after flowering to keep it compact
and encourage fresh growth.

## Pairs with

- Lamb — rosemary and lamb is one of those combinations that exists
  for a reason; in a marinade or pushed into slits in the meat
- Chicken — especially with lemon and garlic in a roast
- Potato — in a gratin, roasted, or as rosemary-infused oil for the pan
- Olive oil — infused slowly for finishing drizzles and dressings
- Orange and citrus — with Cara Cara in cocktails or roasts
- Honey — rosemary honey is excellent with cheese
- Dried beans — a sprig in the cooking water adds background depth

## Where to get it locally

- Own plant
- Widely available in potted herb sections at supermarkets and nurseries;
  starter plants establish quickly

## Notes

**Infused oil:** submerge sprigs in good olive oil and leave for several
days. Strain and use for dressings or finishing. Don't heat — slow infusion
keeps the flavour brighter.

**Drying:** tie small bundles and hang in a dry spot. Dried rosemary keeps
for a year and works well in long-cooked dishes.

**Stripped sprigs as skewers:** remove most of the leaves from thick sprigs,
sharpen the base, and use as skewers for lamb or chicken. The wood imparts
a faint resinous note.

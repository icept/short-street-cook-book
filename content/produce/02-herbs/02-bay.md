A tree, really, that happens to live in the garden. Fresh bay leaves are
a different thing to the dried ones from the supermarket — more complex,
slightly resinous, with a eucalyptus-like green note that you don't get
once the leaf dries. Worth using fresh whenever you can and only reaching
for the jar as a backup.

## In season

Year-round. Leaves can be picked at any time; they're at their most
aromatic in the warmer months when the tree is actively growing.

## Pairs with

- Thyme — the two go together in almost every braise and stock
- Garlic and onion — foundational to most slow-cooked dishes
- Stocks and court bouillons — essential
- Cream-based sauces — bay in a béchamel or a rice pudding is worth the step
- Cured meats — a leaf in the cure or the brine adds depth
- Dried beans and lentils — always add a bay leaf to the cooking water

## Where to get it locally

- Own tree
- Fresh bay rarely sold commercially — dried is the standard; use it
  in double the quantity if substituting for fresh

## Notes

**Fresh vs dried:** use fresh where the bay is a background note (a braise,
a stock). Dried concentrates the flavour differently and is harder to
over-use — but it is not a straight swap.

**Storing fresh:** picked leaves dry naturally at room temperature over
a week or two. Once dry, jar them — they'll last a year and are better
than anything from the supermarket.

**Remove before serving:** bay leaves are not pleasant to eat.

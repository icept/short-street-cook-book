The macadamia is a slow reward — years of doing nothing much before it decides
to produce, and then a glut of nuts hitting the ground all at once. Once you
have them cracked, though, the flavour is worth every bit of the wait. Rich,
buttery, faintly sweet; nothing else quite like them.

## In season

Nuts fall when they're ready, from roughly March through August, with the
main flush in autumn. Don't pick them early — wait for the green husk to split
and the nut to drop on its own. Gather from the ground, husk and all, and
leave them to dry for a week or two before cracking.

## Pairs with

- Honey — raw or dark; the bitterness plays well against macadamia's sweetness
- Brown butter — the classic; toasted nuts in browned butter is hard to improve on
- Chocolate — dark especially, but milk works in baking
- Lime and citrus — cuts the richness, lifts the whole thing
- Roasted vegetables — pumpkin, sweet potato, beetroot; add crunch and fat
- Soft cheese — brie or camembert alongside roasted macadamias and something acidic
- White fish and seafood — in a crust or scattered through a butter sauce
- Caramel and toffee — they hold their texture where other nuts go soft

## Notes

**Cracking:** macadamia shells are among the hardest of any nut — use a
dedicated cracker or a heavy, steady downward pressure. A vice works well in
a pinch. Force without control just sends them across the kitchen.

**Storing:** shelled nuts are high in oil and go rancid quickly at room
temperature. Keep in an airtight container in the fridge and use within a
month, or freeze for longer.

**Roasting:** spread whole on a tray at 160°C for 8–10 minutes, turning once.
Watch closely — they colour fast and burn faster. Pull them when golden; they
crisp further as they cool.

**Macadamia oil:** cold-pressed from the nut, high smoke point, mild flavour.
Good for dressings where you want richness without the flavour of olive oil,
and handles high heat without bitterness.

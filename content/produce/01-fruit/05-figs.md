The fig tree gives two flushes — a small breba crop on last year's wood in
early summer, and the main crop from late summer through autumn. The main crop
is the one worth waiting for: heavy, dark-skinned fruit that splits at the base
when it's ready and tastes of honey and warm afternoons. They don't travel well,
which is why supermarket figs are always a disappointment. Off your own tree,
eaten the day they fall, they're genuinely excellent.

## In season

Main crop February to April. The breba (early) crop is smaller and less
reliable — some years good, some years nothing. Pick when the fruit is soft
and the neck is beginning to bend; a fully ripe fig will pull away from the
branch with almost no resistance.

## Pairs with

- Prosciutto and cured meat — the sweet and the salty is a cliché because
  it works perfectly
- Soft cheese — brie, camembert, gorgonzola, or fresh ricotta alongside
  sliced figs
- Honey and walnuts — roasted figs with honey and walnuts is about as easy
  as a dessert gets
- Balsamic — a good aged balsamic drizzled over halved figs
- Rosemary — in a roasted fig dish or a fig and rosemary jam
- Blue cheese — strong contrast, strong pairing; fig and gorgonzola on a
  flatbread
- Dark chocolate — especially in a tart or alongside poached figs
- Almonds — in a tart or a cake; the nuttiness complements the fig's sweetness

## Where to get it locally

- Own tree
- Fresh figs at **Stirling Farmers Market** (Sundays) in season, usually
  February–March — buy the day you're using them
- Middle Eastern grocers around Northbridge occasionally stock good fresh figs
  in season

## Notes

**Don't refrigerate unripe figs.** Once ripe, they keep a day or two in the
fridge at most.

**Roasting:** halve, drizzle with honey and olive oil, scatter with thyme,
roast at 190°C for 15–20 minutes. Serve warm with cheese or alongside pork.

**Fig jam:** a good use of a glut. Figs are naturally high in pectin — a
simple sugar and lemon juice jam sets reliably without commercial pectin.
Add rosemary or vanilla to the jar while it's hot.

**Drying:** halve and dry in a low oven (70°C) for several hours, or use
a dehydrator. Dried figs store for months and are useful in baking and
on a cheese board.

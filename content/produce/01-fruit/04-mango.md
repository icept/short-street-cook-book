A mango tree in a Perth backyard is a proper gift. Ours comes in over summer —
the fruit bigger than you expect, the flesh so sweet it barely needs anything
done to it. We eat most of them fresh off the tree, but they have plenty of
uses in the kitchen when the glut arrives.

## In season

December through February, with the main flush in January. The fruit is ready
when it gives slightly under thumb pressure and the skin around the stem end
starts to blush. Leave on the tree as long as you can — they develop more
sweetness in the final week. A windfall mango is ripe; pick it up and eat it
that day.

## Pairs with

- Chilli and lime — the classic; heat and acid cut the sweetness perfectly
- Coconut — milk or cream; mango and coconut together is a reliable thing
- Coriander — fresh leaves scattered over, works especially in a salsa or salad
- Fish sauce and sesame — in a dressing or dipping sauce; adds depth and salt
- Sticky rice — Southeast Asian style, with coconut cream
- Fresh ginger — sharp heat that plays well against the sweetness
- Prawn and crab — mango in a seafood salad is hard to argue with
- Dark rum — in a drink or a dessert; tropical and obvious in the best way

## Where to get it locally

- Own tree
- Stirling Farmers Market (Sundays) — WA-grown mangoes from northern WA growers
  in season, usually from mid-December
- Good fruit shops and delis in Perth stock Kensington Pride and R2E2 over
  summer; quality varies — look for Carnarvon or Kununurra fruit if you can

## Notes

**Ripening:** mangoes don't need the fridge until ripe. Leave on the bench
and they'll come good in a day or two if picked slightly firm. Once ripe,
eat within two or three days or refrigerate.

**Cutting:** score around the cheeks with the stone as your guide, then
score the flesh in a grid and push the skin inside out. The hedgehog method
wastes less than trying to peel first.

**Freezing:** peel and cube ripe mango and freeze flat on a tray before
transferring to a bag. Frozen mango is fine in smoothies, sorbet, or
blended into a sauce — not for eating fresh, but better than waste when
the tree drops more than you can use.

**Green mango:** unripe fruit is used in Thai and Vietnamese salads
(som tam style). Very tart, crunchy, works with fish sauce, lime, chilli,
toasted peanuts or cashews.

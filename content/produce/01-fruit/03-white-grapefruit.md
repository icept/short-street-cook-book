Bigger and more assertive than the other citrus in the garden. The white
grapefruit is bitter in the way that forces the cook to make a decision —
it wants something to balance it, whether that's honey, salt, fat, or alcohol.
Left to its own devices it can be bracingly sharp; paired well it becomes
something genuinely interesting.

## In season

July to October, after the oranges have peaked. The later fruit is slightly
sweeter and easier to deal with raw; early-season fruit can be quite bitter
and suits cooking more than eating fresh.

## Pairs with

- Honey — raw or warmed over the cut fruit; the simplest approach
- Sea salt — a pinch over segments, especially with olive oil
- Avocado — grapefruit and avocado in a salad is a reliable thing
- Soft cheese — brie or fresh ricotta with segments and a drizzle of honey
- Gin — the bitterness suits juniper-forward spirits
- Bitter greens — witlof, radicchio, rocket; leans into the bitterness
- Seafood — especially prawns and crab in a citrus-dressed salad
- Brown sugar and vanilla — for a grilled or bruléed grapefruit

## Where to get it locally

- Own tree
- White grapefruit less common at markets; Ruby Red is the more usual
  commercial variety — the flavour is different (sweeter, pinker)

## Used in

## Notes

**Segmenting:** cut away the pith and membrane for salads — use a small,
sharp knife and work over a bowl to catch the juice. The pith of white
grapefruit is thick and quite bitter.

**Grilled grapefruit:** halve, sprinkle with brown sugar, and grill under
a hot grill until caramelised. The heat mellows the bitterness considerably.

**Juice:** the white grapefruit juices well. Strain it and use in dressings
or a Paloma where a Cara Cara would be too sweet.

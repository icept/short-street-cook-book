---
title: Beef Short Rib Massaman
serves: 4
time: 30 minutes, plus 3½ hours in the oven
---

A long, slow braise that rewards patience. Short ribs have enough fat and collagen to stay rich through the full cook without drying out, and the oven does all the work once the pot is sealed.

## Ingredients

- 1 kg beef short ribs
- 2 tbsp neutral oil
- 1 tin (about 200 g) massaman curry paste
- 400 ml coconut milk
- 2 tbsp fish sauce
- 2 tbsp brown sugar
- 1 tbsp tamarind paste
- 2 onions, quartered
- ½ cinnamon stick
- 2 star anise
- dried chilli, to taste
- 500 g waxy potatoes, peeled and cut into large chunks
- salt

## Method

1. Preheat the oven to 145°C. Season the short ribs well with salt.
2. Heat the oil in a large dutch oven over medium-high heat. Brown the ribs on all sides, about 5–6 minutes in total. Remove and set aside.
3. Pour off most of the fat from the pot, leaving about 3 tbsp. Fry the curry paste in the fat over medium heat for 2 minutes until fragrant.
4. Pour in the coconut milk and stir to combine with the paste. Add the fish sauce, brown sugar, and tamarind paste and simmer for 1–2 minutes.
5. Return the ribs to the pot along with the onions, cinnamon stick, star anise, and dried chilli. Add enough water to just cover.
6. Cover with a lid and cook in the oven for 2½ hours.
7. Add the potatoes, replace the lid, and return to the oven for a further hour until the potatoes are soft and the beef is falling from the bone.

## Flavour

Gently spiced rather than hot, with a sweet and sour balance from the palm sugar and tamarind underpinning everything. The cinnamon and star anise sit in the background — present but not dominant. The braising liquid reduces into a sauce that is rich without being heavy.

## Why it works

Short ribs are ideal here because their high collagen content converts to gelatin over the long cook, thickening the sauce naturally and keeping the meat moist. The low oven temperature (145°C) maintains a gentle simmer that coaxes out the collagen without seizing the muscle fibres — a hotter oven would leave the meat dry despite the liquid.
